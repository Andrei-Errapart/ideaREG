## Idea

### Title

**BGA Dummy Load and Loopback Adapters for FPGAs** — commercial product family for power delivery and signal integrity verification on FPGA PCBs.

### Description

This is a product idea — a family of test adapters in BGA packages matching common FPGA footprints, designed for verifying power delivery network performance and signal integrity on PCBs intended for those FPGAs.

The core concept is to replace the actual FPGA with a controlled test adapter during PCB bring-up and characterisation. The adapter shares the FPGA's BGA footprint, can be soldered to the PCB exactly as the FPGA would be, and provides controlled electrical behaviour that allows specific aspects of the PCB's electrical performance to be measured without the complications of running an actual FPGA design.

**Two adapter types:**

**Dummy Load Adapter.** This adapter draws controlled current pulses on the FPGA's power rails. The PCB's DC-DC converters supplying the FPGA see realistic load transients and respond as they would to actual FPGA current draws. The engineer can then measure the supply rail voltage waveforms with an oscilloscope to characterise the transient response, identifying voltage droops, overshoots, oscillations, and other power integrity issues.

The load pulses are externally controlled through **U.FL connectors** on the adapter. A small companion device — connected through U.FL coaxial cables — generates the load pulse control signals, allowing the engineer to specify pulse amplitude, duration, repetition rate, and timing patterns. Multiple load channels can be controlled independently, allowing different power rails (core, IO, transceivers, etc.) to be tested separately or simultaneously.

**Loopback Adapter.** This adapter routes signal pins back to themselves or to other signal pins, allowing PCB signal integrity to be tested without an actual FPGA generating or receiving signals. The loopback may be passive (direct trace routing inside the adapter) or include simple active circuitry for more sophisticated testing patterns. External test equipment can drive signals into the PCB's connectors or transceiver pins, and the loopback adapter routes those signals appropriately for round-trip measurement.

### Why this idea matters

**The need is real.** FPGA designers who care about power integrity and signal integrity at the level of detail that high-performance designs require currently have no good tooling for verifying these aspects of their PCBs. They either build their own test fixtures from scratch each time, use less appropriate tooling, or skip the verification.

**Commercial production is feasible.** The technical requirements are well within standard PCB and packaging technology. BGA packages with custom internal routing exist as commercial products (interposers, redistribution layers, breakout boards). Adding controlled current sources and U.FL connectors to such a package is engineering work but not research.

**Market exists.** FPGA designers working on high-performance, mission-critical, low-noise, or aerospace-grade systems would all benefit. The market is small relative to mass-consumer products, but the per-unit value is high enough that a reasonably priced product could find sustained demand.

**Standard interfaces would multiply the value.** A family of adapters supporting common FPGA footprints (Zynq 7000 series, Zynq UltraScale+, Versal, Stratix, Agilex, Cyclone V SoC, PolarFire SoC, others) with consistent control protocols on the U.FL side would create a reusable test infrastructure that engineers could move between projects.

### Domain or field

FPGA PCB design, power integrity, signal integrity, test and measurement, electronic design automation tooling, PCB bring-up.

### References to related ideas

- **DC-DC converter evaluation boards** with built-in load transient generators (available from various semiconductor vendors for evaluating their own power management ICs).
- **TDR (Time-Domain Reflectometry) probes and test boards** for general PCB signal integrity work.
- **Electronic loads** as standalone instruments (BK Precision, Keysight, others) used for bench testing of power supplies.
- **ATE (Automated Test Equipment) socket adapters** used in semiconductor production testing.

The specific contribution of this idea is the application of these established techniques in a BGA-footprint adapter form factor that solders directly onto the FPGA's intended PCB position, allowing in-situ characterisation of the PCB's power delivery and signal integrity exactly as the FPGA would experience them.

## Remarks

This is a product idea rather than a research idea or a protocol. The intended outcome is for a company — established or new — to develop and sell adapters of this kind as commercial products.

The author of this registration does not currently plan to pursue this product personally. The registration is intended to establish public prior art for the concept and to make the idea freely available for any company that wants to develop it. The MIT license under which this registration is made allows free use, modification, and commercial development by any party.

Specific implementation choices that would need to be made by anyone developing this as a product include:
- Which FPGA footprints to support initially (Zynq 7000 is one explicit candidate; the broader market includes many other footprints).
- How to package the dummy load circuitry inside the BGA constraint (custom silicon, chip-on-board with discrete components, multi-chip module, others).
- The control protocol for the U.FL signals (analog pulse control, digital command interface, mixed signalling).
- The architecture and feature set of the companion control device (simple pulse generator, full programmable test instrument, USB-controlled bench tool, others).
- Pricing and target market positioning.

These are normal product-development decisions and are left to whoever develops the product.

The idea is not fully novel — load transient testing of power supplies is a well-established practice, and BGA-footprint adapters of various kinds exist for other purposes. The specific combination described here, however, is not commercially available as far as the author is aware. Any company that wants to build this product can do so without licensing concerns related to this registration.
