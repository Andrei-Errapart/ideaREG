## Idea

### Title

Attribution infrastructure as the actual problem of AI-assisted authorship

### Description

The contemporary anxiety about AI-assisted writing is mostly framed as a question of authorship: who really wrote this, was it the human or the AI, what is the right credit allocation between them. This framing leads to debates about whether AI-assisted text counts as the human's work, whether it should be disclosed, what percentage of the words came from the AI versus the human, and so on.

This framing is the wrong one for most practical purposes.

The actual problem of AI-assisted authorship is not *who wrote what*, but *how to build attribution infrastructure that documents the collaboration honestly in ways that survive across time and across changes in the underlying tools*.

The shift in framing matters because:

- The *who wrote what* question is unanswerable in any precise sense for most AI-assisted work. The collaboration is too entangled to allow clean credit allocation.
- The *attribution infrastructure* question is answerable. Specific systems can be designed and evaluated. Concrete practices can be developed and adopted.
- Attribution infrastructure that survives across time is independently valuable. It documents the collaboration for future readers who may want to understand how a piece of work was actually made, regardless of whether they care about credit allocation.
- Attribution infrastructure that survives across changes in the underlying tools is necessary because the AI tools available in any given year will be different from the tools available in later years. A system that depends on the specific tools of one moment will fail when those tools are replaced.

The practical implication: people working on AI-assisted authorship should focus less on *should I disclose the AI* and *who deserves credit* questions, and more on *what systems and conventions will let me document this collaboration in ways that future readers will be able to understand and verify*.

The Identor system used in the Antti Bible is one small early example of attribution infrastructure of this kind. The *drafted in collaboration with Claude* footers used on specific entries are another. The explicit AI-authorship attribution on the *Two Bibles* article published on Medium is another. The AiBook category on the GOLC platform is another. These are all early experiments. None is a final answer. The general direction — building attribution infrastructure that handles AI collaboration honestly across long timescales — is the work that matters.

### Why this idea matters

The current debate about AI-assisted writing is consuming substantial intellectual energy on a question that does not have a clean answer (*who wrote what*) while neglecting the question that does have answers (*how do we document the collaboration durably*). Reframing the problem may free that intellectual energy for more productive use.

### Domain or field

Authorship theory, AI ethics, archival practice, attribution systems, intellectual property philosophy.

### References to related ideas

The Identor system used in the Antti Bible.
The standard authorship conventions of academic publishing (which handle multi-author work but not AI collaboration).
Creative Commons licensing schemes (which handle attribution but not collaboration documentation).
Cryptographic timestamping and chain-of-custody systems generally.
